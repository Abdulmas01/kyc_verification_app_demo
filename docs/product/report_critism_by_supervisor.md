II. Evaluation of Core Components
Introduction and Context
Your introduction effectively establishes the industrial need by citing the World Bank (2021) statistic of 1.4 billion unbanked adults globally and the specific underperformance of Nigeria in mobile money adoption (Wezel & Ree, 2023). The regulatory context is well-framed through FATF and NIST SP 800-63-3 references. You correctly identify that rural branch scarcity makes traditional in-person KYC impractical. The linkage between financial exclusion and the absence of affordable remote identity verification is clearly drawn. One minor weakness is that you do not quantify the current cost of manual KYC per verification event in Nigeria, which would strengthen the economic motivation for your proposed solution.
Research Gap
You identify four research gaps, which are generally well-articulated. The first gap “absence of per-task compression sensitivity analysis across a heterogeneous mobile KYC pipeline” is the strongest and most novel. The second gap regarding calibration metrics for KYC decision fusion is also a genuine contribution. However, the third gap (payload tampering in edge-first architectures) seems to me like an engineering design choice than a research gap requiring empirical investigation. The fourth gap (no complete mobile KYC AI pipeline for Nigeria) is broad, it would be stronger if you specified what “complete” means in measurable terms against a defined benchmark or deployment scenario.
Aims and Objectives
Your aim is clearly stated and your six objectives are reasonably specific. Objectives i through iv are measurable and map well to concrete deliverables. However, objective vi (evaluating a calibrated decision engine) depends on the outputs of objectives iii and v, which are still in progress this would create a dependency chain that may compress the available time for the most analytically demanding component. Also, since it depends on objective “v”, its better you make it the last objective. 
Meanwhile, I noticed that none of the objectives are explicitly time-bound, which is a gap against the SMART framework. You should also clarify the success criteria: what accuracy or error thresholds would constitute “acceptable” performance for each module in a deployment context?
Literature Review
Your literature review is competent and covers the relevant thematic areas. You cite foundational works (FaceNet, ArcFace, MobileFaceNet, CelebA-Spoof) and correctly identify their limitations relative to your study. The inclusion of the ArcFace loss equation (Equation 1) is appropriate and aids reader comprehension. However, the review has two notable weaknesses. 
Weaknesses 1: "Descriptive rather than critically synthesized"
What you're currently doing: You're going through papers one by one like a shopping list i.e. "Paper A did X but didn't do Y. Paper B did Z but didn't do W..."
Instead of summarizing papers individually, you should be grouping them by ideas and building a story that leads the reader naturally to why your research is needed.
Weaknesses 2: "No engagement with real-world KYC systems"
You're only citing academic papers, but you're ignoring real systems that companies actually use today, such as:
	•	Jumio, Onfido (commercial tools)
	•	Open-source alternatives
This matters because your research is about KYC pipelines. If you don't acknowledge what already exists in industry, a reader will ask:
"Why build this? Doesn't Jumio already do it?"
You need to compare your work against these systems even if to just say "Jumio does X, but lacks Y, which our system addresses" makes your contribution much more convincing.
Methodology and Experimental Design
Your methodology section is the strongest part of the manuscript. However, there are methodological concerns. 
Problem 1: "Your dataset is too small"
You have 5,000 images split across 5 classes. In deep learning, this is considered quite small. The concern is that your model might have learned to recognise patterns specific to your dataset, not real documents.
Problem 2: "Your images are synthetic/augmented not real"
You used augmented images (artificially modified copies). Will this work on a real photo someone takes with their phone?” You have no real-world test set to prove it does. This is a significant gap because your system is meant to be deployed on real mobile devices with real documents.
Problem 3: "Your speed/latency tests are misleading"
You measured how fast your model runs on Google Colab (CPU), but your app runs on an Android phone, these are completely different environments.
Problem 4: "Face and liveness detection weren't actually tested"
You described how face embedding and liveness detection would work, but you didn't actually run or evaluate them.
III. Analysis, Results, and Data Presentation
Several issues require attention. First, you report a single-point accuracy of 97.73% without any confidence interval or variance estimate. Given the small test set (150 samples per class), you should report 95% confidence intervals using bootstrap resampling or the Wilson interval. Second, the confusion matrix is not presented only summary metrics was. Including a confusion matrix would reveal the specific misclassification patterns between GLARE and GOOD that you mention qualitatively. Third, the INT8 model shows essentially identical file size (5.93 MB vs. 5.94 MB), which is unusual for INT8 quantisation and warrants explanation. Typically, INT8 quantisation should yield approximately 4× weight size reduction; the near-identical sizes suggest that you may have applied dynamic quantisation only to specific layers or that the model size is dominated by non-weight components. This should be explicitly discussed. Fourth, the distilled student achieving higher accuracy (98.67%) than the teacher (97.73%) is a noteworthy result that deserves deeper analysis, while the regularisation explanation citing Hinton et al. (2015) is valid, you should also consider whether the student architecture happens to be better suited to this particular (relatively simple) five-class task.
IV. Holistic Critique and Practical Implications
However, there are several disconnects and unrealistic elements that need addressing. The most significant is the gap between your claimed contribution (per-task compression sensitivity across heterogeneous biometric sub-tasks) and the evidence presented (compression results for only one task). You use phrases such as “the face embedding model is expected to be more sensitive to INT8 rounding errors” you should know that an expectation is not a finding. Until you present empirical compression results for the face embedding and liveness models, your central contribution remains a hypothesis rather than a validated result.
The scalability discussion is thin. You mention a cost estimate of USD 0.04–0.07 per verification but do not show how this was calculated, what server infrastructure assumptions underlie it, or how it scales with concurrent user load. For a system targeting “mass-market users,” this is an important gap. Reliability and failure mode analysis is absent: what happens when the server is unreachable? What is the expected throughput under realistic network conditions in rural Nigeria? What are the graceful degradation mechanisms?
The synthetic-to-real domain gap is insufficiently addressed. You acknowledge this as a limitation in the recommendations, but given that your entire training pipeline is synthetic, this is not a minor limitation but rather a fundamental validity question that should be addressed experimentally, even with a small real-world validation set.

Units and notation are consistent throughout, which is commendable.
VI. Major Criticisms (Top 3 Required Technical Revisions)
1. Incomplete Core Contribution. Your stated central contribution is a per-task compression sensitivity analysis showing that different biometric tasks respond differently to model compression. However, you have only completed compression for the document quality classifier. The face embedding compression (which you yourself identify as the most interesting case due to ArcFace’s angular margin sensitivity) and the liveness detection compression are both missing. You must complete and present compression results for all three sub-tasks before the central claim of the study can be substantiated. Without this, the thesis reduces to a single-task compression study, which is a far weaker contribution.
2. Absence of Real-World Validation Data. The entire training and evaluation pipeline relies on 5,000 synthetically generated document images. While synthetic data generation is a legitimate approach (particularly for privacy-sensitive domains), you have not validated that models trained on synthetic data generalise to real mobile-captured documents. You need to include at least a small real-world validation set (even 200–500 images captured on actual mid-range Android devices under realistic conditions) and report the synthetic-to-real accuracy gap. Without this, the 97.73% accuracy figure is not interpretable in the context of the stated deployment scenario.
3. Missing Uncertainty Quantification and Statistical Rigour. You report single-point accuracy and F1 values without confidence intervals, standard deviations from multiple training runs, or statistical significance tests. Given the small test set (n = 750, 150 per class), stochastic variation in training could meaningfully affect results. You should (a) report 95% confidence intervals for all accuracy and F1 metrics, (b) run the training procedure at least 3–5 times with different random seeds and report mean ± standard deviation, and (c) apply McNemar’s test or a paired bootstrap test to determine whether the accuracy differences between the FP32 baseline, INT8 model, and distilled student are statistically significant.

	•	Table 4.2 reports INT8 model size as 5.93 MB versus the FP32 baseline of 5.94 MB. This negligible difference is inconsistent with typical INT8 compression ratios and should be explained or investigated for a potential reporting error.
	•	The term “server-authoritative” is used throughout but never formally defined. Provide a clear definition on first use.
	•	Equation 1 is rendered in plain text rather than using proper mathematical typesetting. Use an equation editor for clarity and professionalism.
	•	The scope section excludes “structured model pruning” without justification. A brief rationale would strengthen the scoping decisions.
	•	Reference formatting is inconsistent: some entries include DOIs, others do not. Ensure all references with available DOIs include them.
	•	Section 4.3 (Mobile Integration Status) is extremely brief and reads more as a status update than an academic section. Consider either expanding it with technical detail or folding it into the methodology.
	•	The recommendations section uses “Furthermore” and “In addition” as paragraph openers, which is stylistically repetitive.
	•	You do not report GPU training time or total compute cost. Including this information would improve reproducibility and help readers assess resource requirements.
